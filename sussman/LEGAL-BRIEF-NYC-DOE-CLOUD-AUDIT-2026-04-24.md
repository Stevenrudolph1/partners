## III. BACKGROUND

### A. The MNTEST Product

The MNTEST (Multiple Natures Test) is a digital psychometric assessment that:
- Identifies students' natural abilities, personality traits, and academic performance levels
- Makes career pathway recommendations
- Is used by 250,000+ individuals in 18+ countries
- Collects: first/last name, email (optional), 19 personality traits from self-assessment, education plans, and academic performance self-rating

**Privacy Certification**: MNI has been independently audited and certified by **iKeepSafe.org** for compliance with:
- FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act)
- COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)
- GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

iKeepSafe.org is the leading certification authority specifically recognized in the K-12 education sector.

### B. The Sussman-MNI Agreement

In **September 2023**, MNI and Sussman entered into an **Exclusive Licensee & Distribution Agreement** granting Sussman exclusive rights to distribute MNTEST within NYC K-12 public schools.

A core condition of the agreement was that Sussman would:
1. Obtain an NYC DOE software vendor license
2. Navigate the cloud review/audit process required by NYC DOE
3. Secure MNTEST listing in the FAMIS purchasing system

**Sussman's first 6 months** (roughly Sept 2023 – Mar 2024) were consumed largely by obtaining their own vendor software license from NYC DOE — a prerequisite to representing any third-party software in NYC schools.

Sussman also represents Continental Press and Cambridge University Press as resellers of digital educational software through their NYC DOE software contract.

### C. The NYC DOE Cloud Review / ERMA Process

NYC DOE requires software tools that access, collect, or store student information to undergo a cloud review process, known as an **ERMA (Educational Records Management Application) review** or cloud/privacy audit. This process is administered by the NYC DOE's Software Unit (Domion Pontrelli, Kay Robbins) in coordination with their privacy/data security team.

The stated purpose of cloud review is to evaluate:
- Data privacy compliance (FERPA, COPPA, NY Ed Law 2-d)
- Security architecture
- Student data collection and handling
- Third-party data sharing policies

There is **no publicly available timeline, defined procedure, or SLA** governing how long NYC DOE is permitted to take to complete a cloud review. This opacity is itself a source of the problem.

### A. Facts Supporting Unreasonable Delay

1. **18+ months with no decision**: NYC DOE has neither approved nor denied the cloud review. They have neither scheduled an audit nor identified any deficiency that would block one.

2. **MNI is compliant**: iKeepSafe.org certification for FERPA, COPPA, and GDPR demonstrates the product meets or exceeds NYC DOE's stated data privacy standards.

3. **No clear process**: Sussman and MNI were unable to determine from NYC DOE what the process was, who owned it, or what was required. Initial contact was made with one office and redirected multiple times.

4. **Active demand exists**: A district superintendent (Glenda Esperance, D79) has formally and repeatedly requested the product. Multiple schools have expressed interest. NYC DOE's own procurement and school leadership staff are aware of the interest.

5. **Agency confusion**: On November 4, 2025, Kay Robbins asked whether the product had been sent to cloud review and whether it was approved — a question that reveals NYC DOE's own internal records are unclear on the status of their own pending request.

7. **Other similar products are approved**: Ron Sussman references Cloud 9 as a product that passed cloud review under the same Sussman software contract — demonstrating the process can and does complete in a reasonable timeframe.

## VI. LEGAL ANALYSIS

### A. Applicable Law

**Article 78 of the CPLR (Civil Practice Law and Rules)**

Article 78 proceedings allow judicial review of administrative agency action or inaction. Courts may review whether an agency's action was:
- Arbitrary and capricious (no rational basis)
- Contrary to law
- An abuse of discretion

A court may also use Article 78 to compel an agency to act where it has a mandatory duty and has failed to fulfill it in a reasonable timeframe. This is sometimes called a "mandamus-type" Article 78 proceeding.

**New York Freedom of Information Law (FOIL, Public Officers Law §§ 84–90)**

FOIL provides public access to government records. NYC DOE must respond to FOIL requests within 5 business days with either the records or a date for production. Delays can be challenged and may assist in building the pressure case.

**New York Education Law §2-d**

This statute governs student data privacy and requires educational agencies to establish vendor approval processes with clear criteria. Vague, undefined approval processes may conflict with the regulatory intent of §2-d if they create indefinite uncertainty for compliant vendors.

**NYC Administrative Code / Procurement Rules**

NYC DOE operates under procurement rules that govern vendor onboarding timelines and fair vendor treatment. If internal rules or agency policy establish timelines for cloud reviews, failure to follow them may constitute arbitrary conduct.

### B. Potential Claims

**1. Article 78 Proceeding — Compel Agency Action (Mandamus)**

*Theory*: NYC DOE has a duty to process vendor and software review applications within a reasonable time. 18 months of inaction, with no stated basis for delay and no identified deficiencies, is arbitrary and capricious.

*Relief sought*: Court order directing NYC DOE to:
- Complete cloud review within 30 days
- Provide in writing any specific requirements or deficiencies
- If fully compliant, approve FAMIS listing

*Strength*: Moderate. Courts are reluctant to order agencies to act on a specific timeline but will do so when delay is demonstrably unreasonable and documented. The email record here is strong documentation.

*Note*: Must exhaust administrative remedies first — i.e., a formal demand must be made and ignored or rejected before filing.

**2. Article 78 — Arbitrary and Capricious Conduct**

*Theory*: NYC DOE's conduct in this matter — asking the same questions months apart, citing the absence of one employee as a reason for delay, failing to follow defined procedures — constitutes arbitrary and capricious conduct under CPLR §7803(3).

*Relief sought*: Court order annulling any decision to delay and directing immediate processing.

**3. Declaratory Judgment**

*Theory*: MNI may seek a declaration that its product is compliant with applicable privacy standards (iKeepSafe certification) and that NYC DOE is not entitled to indefinitely suspend review without basis.

**4. Damages (longer-term/harder)**

*Theory*: Government entities in New York can be sued for tortious interference or for damages resulting from arbitrary and unreasonable agency inaction, though this is a high bar.

*Challenge*: Damages based on projected revenue ($125K–$350K/year) are vulnerable to being characterized as speculative. Stronger if Superintendent's purchase requests are formalized (written purchase intent from D79, etc.).

*How to strengthen*: Written purchase commitment letters from District 79 and other interested schools would transform speculative projections into quantifiable, documented lost revenue.
