# Scope — Longitudinal Research Partnership

**Status:** Conceptual. Requires MOU, IRB approval, and cohort access commitments before execution.
**Goal:** Publish the first peer-reviewed longitudinal study of Multiple Natures effectiveness with institutional-care youth.

---

## Why This Matters to Both Sides

### For SOS India
- **Advocacy:** Independent research showing measurable effects strengthens the case for MN-based career guidance across SOS operations globally.
- **Funding:** Donors and institutional funders respond to outcome data. "Our students achieve X% higher placement / Y% higher satisfaction when guided by MN" is powerful.
- **Quality of care:** The study itself surfaces insights SOS can use to improve coordinator training and student support.

### For MNI
- **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed evidence is the highest-tier form of validation for the MN framework.
- **Thought leadership:** Places MN in the academic conversation (career psychology, vocational guidance, child welfare research).
- **Defensibility:** When commercial competitors challenge MN's scientific basis, a published study is a direct answer.

### For the broader field
- **Evidence base:** Career guidance for institutional-care youth is understudied. A rigorous longitudinal study contributes to child welfare research broadly.

---

## Hypothesis

**Primary hypothesis:** SOS students whose chosen career path shows alignment with their MNTEST profile (at or above a threshold score) will demonstrate, 3+ years post-placement:
- Higher retention in their chosen field
- Higher self-reported career satisfaction
- Higher earnings stability
- Lower career-switch rates

compared to students whose chosen path diverges from their MNTEST profile.

**Secondary hypotheses:**
- Students who received an MNTEST-informed career conversation with a coordinator show better outcomes than those who took the MNTEST but did not have the conversation.
- Students with certain MN profiles (e.g., Healing-dominant) are more likely to succeed in mismatched careers when specific external supports are in place.

## Cohort

### Available data

| Source | Size | Availability |
|--------|------|--------------|
| Historical MNTEST takers at SOS (2012-present) | Potentially 3,000-5,000+ individuals | Needs SOS confirmation |
| Students assessed via Xavigate Map (SOS Edition, 2026+) | 500-1,000/year going forward | Will be collected as Map program runs |

### Cohort construction

Two concurrent tracks:

**Retrospective cohort (primary publication track):**
- Students who took MNTEST at SOS between 2012-2020
- Now 5-15 years post-assessment — in employment, higher education, or between
- Outcomes collectible via follow-up survey

**Prospective cohort (secondary, long-horizon):**
- Students assessed via Xavigate Map 2026-2030
- Follow-up at 3, 5, 10 years
- Stronger design because both assessment and outcomes are captured systematically

## Variables

### Predictor variables (from MNTEST / Map)
- Nature × Intelligence profile scores (all 9 × 10 cells)
- Career cluster fit (from Map computation)
- Alignment score between profile and chosen path

### Context variables
- Age at assessment
- Gender
- SOS care stage (in-village, youth facility, semi-independent, exited care)
- Documentation status at time of college decision
- Financial band of their funding

### Outcome variables
- Current occupation / field
- Duration in current field
- Self-reported career satisfaction (short instrument, e.g., 5-item scale)
- Earnings band (self-reported, anonymized)
- Changes in field (count, timing)
- Retention indicator (still in the field 3+ years after entry)

### Optional extensions
- Subjective well-being (SWLS or similar short scale)
- Perceived alignment between work and nature (single-item)

## Methodology

### Design
- **Retrospective cohort** with prospective follow-up
- **Sample size target:** 1,000+ completed follow-ups (from the 3,000-5,000 eligible pool)
- **Data collection:** Online survey, phone interview for low-digital-access participants
- **Comparison:** Within-cohort comparison by alignment score tertiles; matched-pair subanalysis where feasible

### Analytic approach
- Descriptive: profile × outcome tables
- Primary: regression models predicting outcomes from alignment score, controlling for context variables
- Secondary: subgroup analyses (by Nature, by care stage, by gender)
- Sensitivity: missing data handling, attrition analysis

### Ethics / IRB
- SOS India's internal research approval process
- Informed consent from participants (or guardians for current minors)
- Data anonymization protocol
- Right to withdraw
- Co-authorship agreements

## Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| Low follow-up response rate (< 20%) | Pilot tests completion rates first; use SOS field staff for outreach; offer small incentive |
| Data quality issues (recall bias, self-report errors) | Triangulate self-report with SOS records where possible; use validated short instruments |
| Null findings (MN shows no effect on outcomes) | Publish anyway — null findings are valuable; the instrument design itself teaches; the replication contributes to the field |
| SOS internal approval delays | Start MOU process early; don't tie commercial agreement to research pace |
| Steven bandwidth | Budget realistically — full study likely requires 200+ hours Steven time over 3 years; outsource statistical work if needed |
| IP / authorship conflicts | Co-authorship order agreed in writing at MOU signing |
| Publication rejection | Have backup journals in mind; preprint on SSRN/PsyArXiv during revision |

- Not owned by MNI alone. SOS India is a full research partner.
